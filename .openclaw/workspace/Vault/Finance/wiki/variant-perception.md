# Variant Perception

A variant perception is a well-founded view about a security's value that differs meaningfully from the market consensus. It is the *source* of alpha: without a variant perception, you are simply buying what everyone else already knows, at a price that already reflects it.

The term is closely associated with hedge fund manager Michael Steinhardt and is explored extensively in [[jack-schwager|Jack Schwager's]] Market Wizards series ([[(To Ingest) YouTube Research - Stock Picking Hedge Fund Strategy|source]]).

## Three patterns of exploitable mispricing

### 1. Bimodal outcomes priced as bell curves

The market's default pricing model assumes a normal distribution of outcomes — a range of moderately good to moderately bad scenarios centered around a consensus estimate. But certain situations are fundamentally **bimodal**: the company either survives and the equity is worth multiples of the current price, or it fails and the equity goes to zero.

When the market applies a bell-curve discount to a bimodal situation, the equity becomes drastically underpriced relative to the probability-weighted expected value. Examples include:
- Companies in litigation where the binary outcome (win/lose) determines viability
- Biotech firms awaiting FDA approval on a single drug
- Restructurings where the question is solvency vs. liquidation

The key analytical task is estimating the *actual* probability of each binary outcome, not the blended "consensus" price ([[(To Ingest) YouTube Research - Stock Picking Hedge Fund Strategy|source]]).

### 2. Free optionality (ignored catalysts)

The market prices a company based solely on its visible, current operations while completely ignoring a future catalyst that has real but uncertain value. You effectively get the hidden asset for free. Examples:

- A retailer whose stock price reflects only store cash flows, ignoring a massive real estate portfolio carried at historical cost
- A legacy tech company priced on declining revenue, ignoring a new product line with explosive growth potential
- A conglomerate where a subsidiary is worth more than the entire enterprise value

The analytical edge comes from identifying and valuing the ignored component. If current operations alone justify the stock price, the catalyst is pure upside ([[(To Ingest) YouTube Research - Stock Picking Hedge Fund Strategy|source]]).

### 3. Price confirmation (thesis + timing)

Develop a fundamental macro or sector thesis first, then **wait for the market's price action to confirm** before entering. This addresses the classic problem of being "right but early" — which, in practice, is indistinguishable from being wrong if it triggers a margin call or psychological capitulation.

Practical application:
- Build the thesis through [[fundamental-analysis-checklist|fundamental analysis]]
- Define what price confirmation looks like (basing pattern, breakout above resistance, relative strength vs. sector)
- Do not catch falling knives — let the stock stabilize and demonstrate that the selling pressure has exhausted itself

This pattern combines fundamental insight with technical discipline ([[(To Ingest) YouTube Research - Stock Picking Hedge Fund Strategy|source]]).

## Relationship to position sizing

The strength of your variant perception should directly inform [[position-sizing|position size]]. A high-conviction variant perception with a clear catalyst and asymmetric payoff structure warrants a larger allocation (within [[kelly-criterion|Half-Kelly]] constraints). A moderate variant perception warrants a starter position at most.
